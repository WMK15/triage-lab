# Physical Symptoms & Triage Acuity Level Mapping

Comprehensive research on how patient-reported symptoms relate to emergency triage levels (KTAS 1–5 / ESI equivalent).

---

## Quick Reference: Symptom-to-Acuity Table

| **Symptom** | **High Acuity (L1–2) Modifiers** | **Typical Range** | **Must-Rule-Out Diagnoses** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Chest Pain** | Acute onset, continuous, diaphoresis, dyspnea, radiation to arm/jaw/back, cardiac risk factors | L2 | ACS, PE, aortic dissection, pericarditis |
| **Dyspnea** | Severe at rest, tripoding, stridor, SpO2 <90%, accessory muscle use | L1–2 | PE, pneumothorax, anaphylaxis, severe asthma, ACS |
| **Abdominal Pain** | Severe, acute, high-risk location (RUQ/RLQ/epigastrium), hemodynamic instability | L2 | Appendicitis, cholecystitis, AAA, ectopic rupture |
| **Headache** | Thunderclap ("worst ever"), sudden severe, age >50, focal neuro signs | L1–2 | SAH, ICH, meningitis, stroke |
| **Altered Mental Status** | Acute change, confusion, lethargy, disorientation | L1–2 | Sepsis, stroke, hypoglycemia, intoxication, TBI |
| **Syncope** | With cardiac risk, palpitations, exertional, hemodynamic instability | L2 | Arrhythmia, structural heart disease, stroke |
| **Fever** | With altered mental status, petechial rash, severe localized infection, hypotension | L1–2 | Sepsis, meningitis, severe pneumonia |
| **Trauma** | High mechanism, GCS <13, LOC, penetrating, unequal pupils | L1–2 | TBI, internal bleeding, spinal injury |
| **Focal Weakness** | Acute asymmetric onset | L1–2 | Stroke, spinal cord compression |
| **Hematemesis** | Large volume, ongoing, hypotension | L1–2 | Upper GI bleed with shock |
| **Stridor** | Acute, respiratory distress | L1–2 | Epiglottitis, anaphylaxis, airway obstruction |
| **Non-blanching Rash + Fever** | Rapidly evolving, petechial/purpuric | L1 | Meningococcal sepsis, meningitis |
| **Suicidal Ideation** | With plan, means, and intent | L1–2 | Imminent self-harm |

## 1. Chief Complaints by Acuity Level

### Level 1 (Resuscitation) — Immediate Life-Saving Intervention NOW

**Presentations:**
- Cardiac or respiratory arrest
- Severe dyspnea with SpO2 <90%, cyanosis, inability to speak
- Unresponsive (GCS ≤ 8)
- Immediate airway intervention needed
- Severe shock (hypotension + altered mental + poor perfusion)
- Thunderclap headache ("worst of life")
- Massive hemorrhage (hematemesis, hemothorax, pulseless)
- Loss of consciousness with trauma
- Severe anaphylaxis with airway involvement

---

### Level 2 (Emergent) — Potential Life Threat, Time-Critical (15-min Assessment)

**Presentations:**
- Chest pain (acute onset, cardiac features: diaphoresis, nausea, SOB, radiation)
- Severe dyspnea (moderate, SpO2 90–94%)
- GCS 9–13 (altered mental status)
- Focal neurological deficit (acute weakness, speech changes, vision loss) → stroke
- Syncope with cardiac risk or recent palpitations
- Severe abdominal pain (acute, severe, high-risk location)
- Fever + signs of serious infection (sepsis features, meningitis signs)
- Hematemesis (any volume with vital instability)
- Stridor (any degree with respiratory effort)
- Severe trauma with high mechanism

**Key feature:** Potential to deteriorate rapidly; not yet in extremis but at high risk.

---

### Level 3 (Urgent) — Potentially Serious, Requires Assessment within 30 min

**Presentations (56% of all ED visits — most common, most difficult to classify):**
- Moderate to severe pain with stable vitals
- Moderate dyspnea (SpO2 >94%, speaks in short sentences)
- Fever in stable adult without sepsis signs
- Head injury with alert patient (GCS 14–15)
- Focal headache, new onset
- Dizziness with some impairment
- Moderate abdominal pain, localized
- Minor head trauma with brief LOC or persistent confusion
- Palpitations without chest pain or significant vital abnormality

**Key feature:** Stable currently but could deteriorate; needs physician evaluation.

---

### Level 4 (Less Urgent) — Stable, Routine Evaluation within 60 min

**Presentations:**
- Mild to moderate pain (1–6/10) with normal vitals
- Stable chronic conditions (chronic cough, stable back pain)
- Mild URI symptoms
- Minor laceration/abrasion
- Mild dizziness in isolated elderly without falls
- Low-grade fever in well-appearing patient
- Stable joint pain

---

### Level 5 (Non-Urgent) — Minimal Acuity, can wait 120 min

**Presentations:**
- Minor wound/laceration requiring cleaning
- Dermatological concern (rash, minor skin condition)
- Prescription refill
- Very mild symptoms

## 2. Organ System Symptom Mapping

### RESPIRATORY SYSTEM

| Symptom | Acuity Indicator | Clinical Context |
|---------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Stridor** (high-pitched inspiratory sound) | L1–2 | **Never benign** — indicates upper airway obstruction (epiglottitis, anaphylaxis, croup with distress, foreign body) |
| **Wheezing** (expiratory/bilateral) | L2 if acute/severe; L3–4 if mild/controlled | Asthma exacerbation, anaphylaxis, pulmonary edema |
| **Cough with hemoptysis** (coughing up blood) | L2 | Pulmonary embolism, tuberculosis, pneumonia, malignancy |
| **Severe dyspnea at rest** | L1–2 | Respiratory failure, anaphylaxis, tension pneumothorax, PE |
| **Moderate dyspnea on exertion** | L3–4 | Stable asthma, deconditioning, mild CHF |
| **Tachypnea > 30/min** | L1–2 | Respiratory distress, severe infection, shock, sepsis |
| **Accessory muscle use** | L1–2 | Respiratory compromise |
| **Tripoding** (sitting forward on arms), **unable to speak full sentences** | L1–2 | Severe respiratory distress |
| **Cyanosis** | L1 | Critical hypoxia |
| **Chest pain + dyspnea** | L2 | ACS, pneumothorax, PE, pericarditis |
| **Dry cough, mild, with sore throat** | L4–5 | URI, likely viral |
| **Productive cough, mild to moderate, no dyspnea** | L3 | Bronchitis, early pneumonia |

---

### CARDIOVASCULAR SYSTEM

| Symptom | Acuity Indicator | Clinical Context |
|---------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Acute chest pain** (sudden, continuous, severe) | L2 | ACS, PE, aortic dissection, pericarditis |
| **Chest pain + diaphoresis + nausea + dyspnea** | L2 | **Classic ACS triad** — highest risk |
| **Chest pain radiating to jaw/arm/back** | L2 | ACS (especially with cardiac risk factors) |
| **Chest "pressure," "heaviness," "tightness"** | L2 | ACS |
| **Palpitations + syncope** | L2 | Arrhythmia with hemodynamic compromise, structural heart disease |
| **Palpitations + chest pain + dyspnea** | L2 | Arrhythmia with hemodynamic instability |
| **Syncope** (sudden loss of consciousness) | L2 | Cardiac arrhythmia, structural disease, neurological (seizure, TIA) |
| **Presyncope** (feeling faint, no LOC) | L2–3 | Arrhythmia, orthostatic hypotension, cardiac disease |
| **Sudden severe abdominal/back pain + hypotension** | L1 | **AAA (abdominal aortic aneurysm)** — highest mortality if missed |
| **Shock** (hypotension + altered mental + poor perfusion) | L1 | Life-threatening (septic, cardiogenic, hemorrhagic) |
| **Acute severe hypertension** | L2 | Hypertensive emergency, stroke risk |
| **Chronic stable chest pain** | L4–5 | Likely musculoskeletal if normal vitals |

**Critical Note on Women with ACS:** Women present atypically with pain **between shoulder blades**, **nausea/vomiting**, **dyspnea**; less likely to have chest pain or diaphoresis. Do **not** undertriage women with dyspnea, nausea, or arm/jaw pain.

### NEUROLOGICAL SYSTEM

| Symptom | Acuity Indicator | Clinical Context |
|---------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Thunderclap headache** ("worst of life," sudden maximal intensity) | L1 | **SAH (subarachnoid hemorrhage)** — can be lethal in minutes |
| **Sudden severe headache + neck stiffness + fever** | L1–2 | Meningitis, meningococcal sepsis |
| **Sudden severe headache + focal neuro signs** (weakness, vision loss, speech difficulty) | L1–2 | ICH, SAH, stroke |
| **New severe headache (age >50, first occurrence)** | L2 | Must exclude serious pathology (temporal arteritis, malignancy, hypertensive emergency) |
| **Headache triggered by coughing/sneezing** | L2 | Suggests increased intracranial pressure, possible mass |
| **Headache + vision changes + weakness** | L2 | Stroke, reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome |
| **Altered mental status** (acute confusion, disorientation) | L1–2 | **Sepsis, stroke, hypoglycemia, drug intoxication, head injury, encephalitis** — all critical |
| **Lethargy/decreased responsiveness** | L1–2 | Critical illness |
| **Focal weakness** (asymmetric, acute onset) | L1–2 | **STROKE** — assess for thrombolytic window (4.5 h for IV tPA, 24 h for thrombectomy in some cases) |
| **Speech difficulty** (acute) | L1–2 | Stroke |
| **Vision loss** (acute, any field) | L1–2 | Stroke (retinal artery occlusion), other retinal emergencies |
| **Facial droop** | L1–2 | **STROKE** (FAST positive) |
| **True vertigo** (spinning sensation) | L3–4 if benign peripheral (BPPV), L2 if new with other neuro signs | Must differentiate central (dangerous) vs. peripheral (often benign); new vertigo high-risk until proven otherwise |
| **Dizziness + TIA warning signs** (any focal neuro, speech, vision change) | L2 | **TIA** — 50% stroke risk within 90 days, 25% within 48 hours |
| **Seizure activity** (ongoing or post-ictal) | L1–2 | Assess for precipitant (infection, head injury, metabolic, withdrawal) |
| **Loss of consciousness** | L1 | Life-threatening |
| **Migraine with aura, recurrent pattern** | L4 | If same as usual; if different, escalate to L2 |
| **Mild dizziness, isolated, no neuro signs** | L4–5 | Likely benign if vitals stable |

**Stroke Red Flags (FAST assessment — ANY positive = L1–2):**
- **F** = Facial droop
- **A** = Arm weakness
- **S** = Speech difficulty
- **T** = Time to thrombolytics (call it in!)

### GI/ABDOMINAL SYSTEM

| Symptom | Acuity Indicator | Clinical Context |
|---------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Severe acute abdominal pain** (sudden, severe, worst pain) | L2 | Appendicitis, perforated viscus, AAA, ectopic rupture, acute mesenteric ischemia |
| **Severe abd pain + hypotension + tachycardia** | L1 | Hemorrhage, severe peritonitis, septic shock |
| **Acute abd pain + vomiting + unable to keep down fluids** | L2 | Obstruction, peritonitis |
| **Abd pain radiating to back** | L2 | **AAA, pancreatitis** — highest mortality |
| **Right upper quadrant (RUQ) pain + fever + vomiting** | L2 | Cholecystitis (acute cholecystitis), cholangitis |
| **Right lower quadrant (RLQ) pain** (acute onset) | L2–3 | Appendicitis (classic), mesenteric adenitis (viral) |
| **Epigastric pain radiating to shoulder** | L2 | AAA, splenic rupture (if trauma) |
| **Hematemesis** (vomiting blood) | L2 | Upper GI bleed — requires resuscitation |
| **Large volume hematemesis** | L1 | Severe hemorrhage, risk of aspiration/airway compromise |
| **Melena** (black tarry stool, indicating upper GI bleed) | L2–3 | Upper GI bleed |
| **Hematochezia** (bright red blood PR, lower GI) | L3–4 | Lower GI bleed (less immediately life-threatening than upper) |
| **Abd pain + bloody diarrhea + fever** | L2–3 | Inflammatory bowel disease, infectious colitis, toxic megacolon risk |
| **Constant abd pain, worsening, progressive** | L2 | Suggests acute process, peritoneal involvement |
| **Colicky abd pain** (intermittent, comes and goes) | L3–4 | Suggests obstruction/kidney stone, less urgent than constant |
| **Abd pain worsening with movement** | L2 | Peritoneal involvement (peritonitis) |
| **Abd pain improved by lying still, not moving** | L4 | Suggests less acute pathology |
| **Nausea/vomiting alone** (no abd pain) | L3–4 | GI or metabolic cause; assess for other red flags (chest pain, headache) |
| **Projectile vomiting** | L2–3 | Suggests obstruction, raised ICP |
| **Persistent vomiting unable to tolerate fluids** | L3 | Dehydration risk |
| **Mild crampy abd discomfort, painless diarrhea** | L4–5 | Likely viral gastroenteritis |

### TRAUMA/INJURY

| Indicator | Acuity | Clinical Context |
|-----------|--------|------------------|
| **High-speed MVC (>40 mph), ejection, death of occupant** | L1–2 | High-risk injury mechanism |
| **Fall >10–20 feet, pedestrian struck by vehicle, motorcycle crash** | L1–2 | Significant trauma |
| **GCS ≤ 8** | L1 | Severe head injury, airway compromise |
| **GCS 9–12** | L2 | Moderate head injury |
| **GCS 13–15** (mild injury — but if high-risk mechanism, consider L2) | L3–4 | Mild injury; mechanism determines final triage |
| **Loss of consciousness** (any duration) | L1–2 | TBI risk, needs imaging |
| **Unequal pupils** | L1 | Intracranial emergency (epidural hematoma, increased ICP) |
| **Clear fluid from nose/ear** (CSF leak) | L1–2 | Basilar skull fracture |
| **Open/penetrating wound to head, neck, or trunk** | L1–2 | Life-threatening |
| **Obvious deformity/angulation** | L2–3 | Fracture/dislocation |
| **Severe bleeding** (arterial, uncontrolled) | L1 | Hemorrhage, airway risk |
| **Pale, cold, diaphoretic post-trauma** | L1 | Shock, internal bleeding |
| **Abdominal trauma + tachycardia + hypotension** | L1 | Internal bleeding |
| **Chest trauma + respiratory distress + hypoxia** | L1 | Pneumothorax, hemothorax, pulmonary contusion, tamponade |
| **Penetrating extremity wound** | L2–3 | Depends on size, proximity to vital structures |
| **Minor laceration/abrasion, well-controlled bleeding** | L4–5 | Low-risk |

**Geriatric Trauma Undertriage Risk:** Elderly (>65) are systematically undertriaged with standard mechanisms. Consider **lower threshold for L1–2** in elderly with trauma.

### INFECTION/FEVER

| Symptom Pattern | Acuity Indicator | Clinical Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Fever (>38.3°C) alone in well-appearing patient** | L4–5 | Likely viral URI, low-risk |
| **Fever + altered mental status** | L1–2 | **Sepsis, meningitis, encephalitis** — emergency |
| **Fever + non-blanching petechial/purpuric rash** | L1 | **MENINGOCOCCAL SEPSIS** — kills in hours; emergency |
| **Fever + neck stiffness + headache** | L1–2 | **Meningitis** — lumbar puncture/antibiotics needed urgently |
| **Fever + severe localized pain** (flank, RUQ, etc.) | L2 | Pyelonephritis, cholecystitis, other localized infection |
| **Fever + severe dyspnea** | L2 | Pneumonia (especially if tachypnea >30, hypoxia) |
| **Fever + altered mental + tachycardia + tachypnea** | L1–2 | **SEPSIS** — meets SIRS criteria; high mortality |
| **Fever + hypotension + poor perfusion (mottled skin, delayed cap refill)** | L1 | **SEPTIC SHOCK** — highest mortality |
| **Chills** | L2 | Suggests systemic infection (bacteremia) |
| **Lymphadenopathy + fever** | L3 | Viral infection, EBV, early lymphoma (assess for respiratory symptoms) |
| **Immunocompromised + any fever** | L2 | Higher sepsis risk; treat as high-acuity |
| **Low-grade fever (37.5–38°C) in well-appearing patient** | L4–5 | Likely viral |

**Critical:** Elderly may **not have fever** even with severe infection. They present with only confusion, fatigue, weakness, anorexia. **Undertriage common in geriatric sepsis.**

### PSYCHIATRIC/BEHAVIORAL

| Symptom | Acuity Indicator | Clinical Context |
|---------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Suicidal ideation with plan and intent** | L1–2 | Imminent self-harm risk |
| **Suicidal ideation + access to means** | L1–2 | High imminent risk |
| **Homicidal ideation** | L1–2 | Danger to others |
| **Severe agitation** | L2 | Risk of harm to self/others |
| **Hallucinations + confusion + disorganized behavior** | L2 | Psychosis, delirium |
| **Acute behavior change** | L2 | **May indicate medical emergency** (infection, intoxication, metabolic, neuro) |
| **Aggressive/violent behavior** | L2 | Safety concern; medical workup needed (delirium from infection, hypoglycemia, etc.) |
| **Acute mania** | L3 | High-risk behavior possible |
| **Passive suicidal ideation** (no plan, no intent) | L3–4 | Requires assessment; lower immediate risk |

**Key:** Any acute behavioral change must prompt **medical workup** (glucose check, vitals, neuro exam) to rule out delirium from medical cause (sepsis, hypoglycemia, stroke, intoxication).

## 3. Symptom Severity Modifiers

**Phrases/Descriptors Indicating HIGH Acuity:**
- "Worst pain/headache of my life" → **L2 minimum**
- "Came on like a ton of bricks" / "Sudden onset" → **L2 until proven otherwise**
- "Can't move" / "Can't breathe" / "Can't swallow" → **L1–2**
- "Severe" / "Unbearable" / "Intense" → Escalate one level
- "Rapidly worsening" / "Getting worse by the minute" → **L2**
- "Never happened before" / "Different from usual" → **L2** (new symptoms are high-risk)
- "Radiating" (pain) → **L2–3**, especially chest/abdominal pain
- "Diaphoresis" / "Sweating profusely" → **L2** (sign of severe distress/shock)
- "Can barely move" / "Completely unable to function" → **L2–3**

**Phrases Suggesting Lower Acuity:**
- "Chronic" / "Long-standing" / "For weeks/months" → **L3–5**
- "Like my usual headache/back pain" → **L3–4** (if no red flags)
- "Mild" / "Just uncomfortable" → **L4–5**
- "Getting better" → **L4–5**
- "Controlled with OTC meds" → **L4–5**

## 4. Pain Score & Acuity Relationship

**Important:** Pain score alone is not a reliable triage indicator and can cause **overtriage.**

| Pain Score (NRS 0–10) | Typical Acuity | **BUT context matters** |
|---|---|---|
| 0–3 | L4–5 | Mild pain |
| 4–6 | L3–4 | Moderate pain; escalate if vital abnormality, high-risk location |
| 7–10 | L2–3 | Severe pain; escalate to L2 if vital abnormality, acute onset, high-risk location |

**Critical Caveat:** 
- Pain score averages **7.36/10 at triage** vs. **5.5/10 for observer-rated** — patients systematically inflate their own pain scores
- Severe pain **without vital sign abnormality** should not automatically be L2
- Pain in elderly may be **minimized** despite serious underlying pathology
- Stoic patients or medical professionals may **downplay pain** despite serious conditions
- Always assess **facial expression, body language, vital signs** to cross-validate pain report

**KTAS Finding:** Incorporating raw pain scores **reduced** triage accuracy (AUC 0.765 → 0.736). Use pain as a **context modifier**, not a standalone predictor.

**Better approach:** Pain × (location + character + vital context)
- Central acute pain 7+ → escalate
- Peripheral chronic pain 8+ → use vital signs and clinical context to decide

## 5. Vital Signs & Acuity Correlation

**All patients at triage MUST have vital signs measured (ESI mandate) before assigning L3–5.**

### Critical Vital Sign Thresholds

| Vital Sign | Normal | Elevated Acuity | Critical (L1) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SpO2** | >94% RA | 88–94% (L2–3) | <88% |
| **Heart Rate** | 60–100 bpm | >100 (L2–3), >120 (L2) | <40 or >150 |
| **Respiratory Rate** | 12–20 bpm | 21–30 (L2–3), >30 (L2) | <8 or >40 |
| **Systolic BP** | 90–140 mmHg | <90 (L1), >180 (L2) | <70 or >200 with symptoms |
| **Diastolic BP** | 60–90 mmHg | >110 (L2) | — |
| **Temperature** | 37±1°C | >38.3°C or <36°C in context (L2–3) | — |
| **GCS** | 15 (alert × 3) | 14 (mild impairment), 9–13 (L2) | 3–8 (L1) |

### Critical Principle: **Normal Vital Signs ≠ Low Acuity**

- Early sepsis, ACS, stroke can present with **normal vitals**
- Patients on beta-blockers may not show expected tachycardia despite shock
- Elderly have blunted physiological responses — lower thresholds for concern
- **ESI Algorithm:** Measure vitals before assigning L3–5 to catch these high-acuity patients

### RED FLAG Vital Signs (even without other symptoms = escalate to L2)

- **Hypotension <90/60** → L1–2 (shock)
- **Severe tachycardia >130** → L2 (arrhythmia, shock)
- **Severe tachypnea >35** → L2 (respiratory distress)
- **Hypoxia <88%** → L1–2
- **Fever >40°C or hypothermia <36°C** → L2–3 (suggests severe illness)
- **Any altered mental status** → L1–2

## 6. High-Risk Presentations (Auto-Escalate to L1–2)

**These symptoms automatically trigger L1–2 regardless of appearance or vital signs:**

| Symptom | Why | Must-Rule-Out |
|---|---|---|
| **Thunderclap headache** | SAH lethal in minutes if untreated | Subarachnoid hemorrhage |
| **Acute severe chest pain** | ACS high mortality without urgent treatment | MI, PE, aortic dissection |
| **Sudden focal neuro deficit** | Stroke window is 4.5–24 hours depending on treatment available | Acute ischemic stroke, ICH |
| **Syncope with cardiac risk** | Sudden cardiac death risk | Arrhythmia, structural heart disease |
| **Severe dyspnea at rest** | Respiratory failure imminent | PE, pneumothorax, anaphylaxis, severe asthma |
| **Acute altered mental status** | Multiple life-threatening causes; high mortality | Sepsis, stroke, hypoglycemia, intoxication, TBI |
| **Hematemesis** | Rapid blood loss risk | Upper GI bleed |
| **Non-blanching petechial rash + fever** | Meningococcal disease kills in hours | Meningococcal sepsis, meningitis |
| **Stridor** | Upper airway obstruction is life-threatening | Epiglottitis, anaphylaxis, foreign body |
| **Acute severe abd pain + hypotension** | Hemorrhage or peritonitis with shock | AAA, ruptured ectopic, perforated viscus |

## 7. Frequently Undertriaged Presentations

**High-risk for being assigned lower acuity than warranted:**

1. **Women with ACS** — atypical presentations (dyspnea, nausea, back pain without chest pain) missed
2. **Elderly with sepsis** — may present with only confusion, fatigue, no fever, subtle infection signs
3. **Stroke in young patients** — less expected; often missed
4. **"Dizziness" masking stroke** — patient labeled vertigo/vestibular when actually TIA
5. **Syncope** — often triaged as fainting, missing serious cardiac disease
6. **Subtle neuro signs** — subtle weakness, speech changes mistaken for anxiety
7. **Severe pain without vital abnormality** — pain 7+/10 may be dismissed if vitals normal
8. **Silent MI in diabetics** — present with only dyspnea, weakness, fatigue; no chest pain
9. **Pediatric sepsis with lethargy** — child appearing "sleepy" is actually altered mental status
10. **Suicidal ideation without clear plan** — often undertriaged as low-risk

## 8. Age & Sex Differences in Symptom Presentation

### Elderly (>65 years) — ATYPICAL PRESENTATIONS

**ACS in elderly:**
- Presentation: dyspnea, fatigue, weakness, nausea — **NO chest pain**
- Older age = worse outcomes due to undertriage

**Sepsis in elderly:**
- Presentation: confusion, anorexia, fatigue, weakness — **may have no fever**
- Immunosenescence: blunted fever response; absence of fever ≠ absence of infection
- Higher mortality from delayed recognition

**Stroke in elderly:**
- Presentation: vague malaise, falls, confusion rather than classic weakness/speech changes
- Often attributed to "baseline"

**Trauma in elderly:**
- High undertriage risk: significant injuries from minor mechanisms (fall from standing)
- Medications: anticoagulants increase bleed risk; beta-blockers mask tachycardia

**Triage implications:**
- **Lower threshold for L2** in elderly with nonspecific complaints + any vital abnormality
- Don't attribute confusion to "baseline dementia" — consider delirium from infection, stroke, MI
- Polymedication: medications may mask typical presentation (beta-blockers, anticholinergics)

### Women — ACS, Stroke, Sepsis

**ACS Presentation Differences:**
- Higher odds of: back/shoulder blade pain, nausea/vomiting, dyspnea, palpitations, jaw pain
- Lower odds of: classic chest pain, diaphoresis, "heavy pressure"
- **Longer delays to treatment** due to atypical symptom pattern
- Post-menopausal: higher MI mortality

**Stroke Presentation:**
- Similar acute focal deficits but may present with vertigo (different complaint than weakness)

**Sepsis:**
- Pregnant/postpartum women at high risk
- Gynecologic infection (ectopic rupture, septic abortion, PID) can masquerade as abdominal pain

**Triage approach:** Do **NOT** dismiss women's chest pain/dyspnea as anxiety or atypical. ACS in women has worse outcomes partly due to undertriage.

### Pediatric (Ages 0–18)

| Age Group | Red Flags | Acuity Modifiers |
|---|---|---|
| **Neonates (0–28d)** | Fever (even <38.5°C), poor feeding, lethargy, jaundice | Any fever = L2; high sepsis risk |
| **Infants (1–12m)** | Fever <3m is L2; altered mental status, apnea, stridor | Assess perfusion, hydration status |
| **Toddlers (1–3y)** | Stridor (croup common but epiglottitis possible), fever + lethargy | Children can't describe; use observation |
| **School-age (4–12y)** | Abdominal pain (less localizing), new seizure, severe headache | Higher threshold for appendicitis; viral causes common |
| **Adolescents (13–18y)** | Similar to adults; assess psychiatric/behavioral emergencies, pregnancy | Suicide risk significant in teens |

**Pediatric Assessment:** 
- **Pediatric Assessment Triangle:** Appearance (consciousness, perfusion), Breathing (effort, sounds), Circulation (skin color, perfusion)
- **High-acuity children may appear deceptively well initially**
- **Lethargy** (not just crying) = altered mental status = **L2**
- **Stridor** = assess airway; children deteriorate rapidly

## 9. Symptom Clusters & Red Flag Combinations

**Dangerous Symptom Combinations (Multiple Symptoms Together = Higher Acuity):**

| Combination | Suggested Diagnosis | Acuity |
|---|---|---|
| **Weakness + Fatigue** | High in-hospital mortality | L2 |
| **Weakness + Headache** | Mortality risk OR 3.01 | L2 |
| **Sudden severe abd pain + radiating to back + dizziness + hypotension** | AAA (abdominal aortic aneurysm) | L1 |
| **Chest pain + diaphoresis + nausea + dyspnea** | ACS | L2 |
| **Fever + petechial non-blanching rash + headache + neck stiffness** | Meningococcal sepsis | L1 |
| **Fever + neck stiffness + headache** | Meningitis | L1–2 |
| **Facial droop + arm weakness + speech difficulty** (acute) | Stroke | L1–2 |
| **Stridor + respiratory distress + drooling** | Upper airway obstruction | L1–2 |
| **Altered mental status + tachycardia + tachypnea + fever** | Sepsis | L1–2 |
| **Agitation + confusion + rapid progression + hypoxia** | Severe illness (sepsis, pneumonia) | L1–2 |

**Symptom Onset Matters:**
- **Sudden onset** = higher acuity (thunderclap headache, sudden weakness, acute chest pain) → L1–2 until proven otherwise
- **Gradual/progressive** = may indicate chronic BUT concerning if worsening (progressive weakness, progressive dyspnea, progressive confusion)
- **Duration context:** Minutes (highest acuity), Hours (high acuity if severe), Days (often lower acuity UNLESS severe pain, vital instability, or red flags)

## 10. Clinical Pearls for Accurate Triage

1. **Do NOT rely solely on vital signs.** Normal vitals do NOT equal low acuity. Early sepsis, ACS, stroke all present with normal vitals.

2. **"Sudden onset" almost always = L2.** Thunderclap headaches, sudden weakness, acute chest pain — all high-risk until proven.

3. **Symptom clusters matter more than single symptoms.** Weakness + headache = 3× mortality risk vs. either symptom alone.

4. **Trust descriptive language.** "Worst pain/headache of my life" = L2 minimum. "Radiating," "severe," "unbearable," "sudden" = escalate.

5. **Elderly with vague complaints = high suspicion.** "Don't feel right," fatigue, confusion may be sepsis, ACS, stroke without classic presentation.

6. **Women with dyspnea/nausea ≠ anxiety.** ACS presents atypically in women; high mortality from undertriage.

7. **Syncope is NOT a diagnosis; it's a symptom of serious disease.** Always assess cardiac risk, mechanism, associated symptoms.

8. **Altered mental status is L1–2 until proven otherwise.** Medical causes (sepsis, hypoglycemia, stroke) must be ruled out.

9. **Fever without source + any other concerning sign = L2.** Don't assume simple viral illness; must assess for serious infection.

10. **High-risk medications (anticoagulants) + minor complaints = higher acuity.** Patient on warfarin with dizziness has higher bleed risk.
