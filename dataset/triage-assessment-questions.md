# Triage Assessment: Structured Question Framework

A progressive questioning strategy to accurately determine patient acuity — moving from broad chief complaint to specific red flags, vital sign context, and severity indicators.

---

## Overview

**Goal:** Extract accurate symptom information to classify into KTAS levels 1–5.

**Principle:** Ask in a funnel approach:
1. **Broad opening question** — what brings you in?
2. **Onset & timing** — when did this start? How fast?
3. **Associated symptoms** — what else are you experiencing?
4. **Severity descriptors** — how bad is this?
5. **Red flags & systems review** — screening for high-risk features
6. **Vital sign context** — has anything made you feel worse?
7. **Risk factors & history** — do you have pre-existing conditions that matter?

---

## Phase 1: Chief Complaint (Broad Opening)

**Goal:** Identify the primary presenting symptom in the patient's own words.

### Question Set 1.1: Initial Presentation

1. **"What brings you to the emergency department today?"**
   - Listen for chief complaint without interruption
   - Note how they describe it (severity, distress level in their voice)
   - Document exact wording when possible

2. **"Is there one main problem, or are there multiple things bothering you?"**
   - Identify primary vs. secondary complaints
   - If multiple, ask them to prioritize: "Which is most concerning to you right now?"

3. **"How long have you been having [chief complaint]?"**
   - Duration (minutes, hours, days, weeks, months)
   - **Red flag onset:** Minutes or hours = higher acuity
   - Chronic onset = lower acuity (unless acute change)

4. **"Did this come on suddenly, or did it start gradually?"**
   - Sudden onset = **typically higher acuity** (L1–2 until proven otherwise)
   - Gradual onset = often lower acuity (unless progressive/worsening)

5. **"Have you ever had anything like this before?"**
   - If yes: "Is this the same as your usual [symptom]?"
   - **New symptom = higher acuity** (unpredictable, unknown cause)
   - **Same as usual = may be lower acuity** (if chronically stable)

---

## Phase 2: Onset, Timing, and Progression

**Goal:** Establish acuity based on speed of symptom development.

### Question Set 2.1: Timeline and Progression

1. **"When exactly did this start? What were you doing?"**
   - Precise timing (happened at work? woke up with it? during activity?)
   - **Activity-triggered = context matters** (chest pain with exertion = ACS risk)

2. **"Has it gotten worse, better, or stayed the same since it started?"**
   - Rapidly worsening = **L1–2**
   - Stable = lower acuity risk, but still evaluate severity
   - Improving = lower acuity (unless severe currently)

3. **"Is it constant, or does it come and go?"**
   - Constant pain = typically more serious than intermittent
   - Intermittent (colicky) = suggests obstruction/cramping (moderate acuity)
   - Intermittent but occasional severe episodes = high-risk

4. **"If it comes and go, how often? And how long does each episode last?"**
   - High frequency + long duration = higher acuity
   - Rare, brief episodes = lower acuity

5. **"What makes it better or worse? Does anything trigger it or relieve it?"**
   - Worse with movement = peritoneal involvement (higher acuity)
   - Worse with activity = cardiac/respiratory concern
   - Relieved by rest = lower acuity
   - Relieved by OTC meds = lower acuity
   - **Nothing helps = higher acuity**

---

## Phase 3: Associated Symptoms (Systems Review)

**Goal:** Identify symptom clusters that suggest serious underlying conditions.

### Question Set 3.1: Respiratory Symptoms

**Ask if:** Shortness of breath, chest pain, or respiratory complaint present.

1. **"Are you having any trouble breathing right now?"**
   - Yes = assess severity next; No = continue to cardiovascular

2. **"Can you speak in full sentences, or do you get short of breath talking?"**
   - Cannot speak full sentences = **respiratory distress, L1–2**
   - Speaks in short sentences = moderate distress (L2–3)
   - Full sentences comfortably = mild/no respiratory distress

3. **"Is your breathing fast, slow, or normal?"**
   - Patient assessment of respiratory rate (not precise, but indicates concern)

4. **"Do you hear any wheezing or whistling when you breathe?"**
   - Wheezing = asthma, anaphylaxis (moderate to high acuity)

5. **"Do you cough? If yes, what does it sound like? Any blood?"**
   - Hemoptysis = high acuity (PE, infection, malignancy)
   - Productive vs. dry cough = context for infection vs. irritant

---

### Question Set 3.2: Cardiovascular Symptoms

**Ask if:** Chest pain, palpitations, syncope, or dyspnea present.

1. **"Are you having any chest pain or chest discomfort?"**
   - Yes = proceed to characterization; No = continue

2. **"Can you describe the pain? Is it sharp, dull, pressure, tightness, heaviness?"**
   - **Pressure, heaviness, tightness = ACS risk, L2**
   - Sharp = usually pleuritic (musculoskeletal, pericarditis)
   - Dull = can be ACS or other causes

3. **"Where exactly is the pain? Does it stay in one place or move around?"**
   - Central chest = highest ACS risk
   - Radiating to arm, jaw, shoulder blade, back = **ACS, aortic dissection, L2**
   - Localized lateral = musculoskeletal (lower acuity)

4. **"How severe is the pain on a scale of 0–10, with 10 being the worst pain you've ever felt?"**
   - 7+ = escalate acuity
   - Associated with vital abnormality = definitely L2

5. **"Are you having any of the following: sweating, nausea, shortness of breath?"**
   - **Any of these with chest pain = ACS triad, L2**

6. **"Have you ever had a heart attack, heart problems, or high cholesterol?"**
   - Cardiac risk factors = elevate concern even with atypical presentation

7. **"Do you feel your heart racing or have palpitations?"**
   - Palpitations + chest pain = arrhythmia (L2)
   - Palpitations alone, stable vitals = lower acuity

8. **"Have you fainted or felt like you were going to faint?"**
   - **Syncope = L2 until cardiac cause ruled out**
   - Presyncope (near-fainting) = assess cardiac risk

---

### Question Set 3.3: Neurological Symptoms

**Ask if:** Headache, dizziness, weakness, confusion, or change in consciousness noted.

1. **"Are you having any headache?"**
   - Yes = proceed; No = continue

2. **"On a scale of 0–10, how bad is the headache? Is it the worst headache you've ever had?"**
   - **"Worst ever" = thunderclap until proven otherwise, L1–2**
   - 7+ = high-risk headache (L2)
   - Matches prior migraines = lower risk if same pattern

3. **"When did it start? Was it sudden or gradual?"**
   - **Sudden onset = higher acuity**
   - Gradual = usually lower acuity

4. **"Do you have any neck stiffness?"**
   - **Neck stiffness + fever + headache = meningitis, L1–2**

5. **"Have you had any vision changes, confusion, weakness, or speech difficulty?"**
   - **Any focal neuro deficit = stroke, L1–2** (assess for thrombolytic window)

6. **"Are you feeling confused or having trouble thinking clearly?"**
   - **Acute confusion = L2 until serious cause ruled out** (sepsis, stroke, hypoglycemia, intoxication)

7. **"Are you more tired/sleepy than usual, or having trouble staying awake?"**
   - **Lethargy = altered mental status, L1–2**
   - Normal alertness = reassuring

8. **"Are you having any dizziness or spinning sensation?"**
   - Vertigo (true spinning) = may be benign BPPV or serious (stroke)
   - Dizziness (lightheadedness) = orthostatic, dehydration, or cardiac

9. **"Have you had any weakness or trouble moving?"**
   - **Acute weakness = stroke risk, L1–2**
   - Chronic weakness = lower acuity if stable

10. **"Any numbness or tingling?"**
    - Recent onset + neurological = stroke/TIA (L2)

11. **"Have you had any seizures or loss of consciousness?"**
    - **LOC = L1–2**
    - Seizure activity = L1–2 (assess for precipitant)

---

### Question Set 3.4: Abdominal/GI Symptoms

**Ask if:** Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, or bleeding present.

1. **"Are you having any belly or abdominal pain?"**
   - Yes = proceed; No = continue

2. **"Where exactly is the pain? Can you point to it?"**
   - **RUQ = cholecystitis/cholangitis (L2)**
   - **RLQ = appendicitis (L2–3)**
   - **Epigastric = MI, pancreatitis, ulcer (L2)**
   - **Central = obstruction, appendicitis (L2–3)**
   - **Left lower = colitis, diverticulitis (L2–3)**
   - Diffuse = peritonitis or generalized process (L2)

3. **"Is the pain sharp, dull, cramps, or constant?"**
   - Sharp constant = high-risk
   - Crampy = obstruction (L3)
   - Dull = variable acuity

4. **"Does the pain stay in your belly, or does it radiate anywhere?"**
   - **Radiating to back = AAA, pancreatitis (L1–2)**
   - **Radiating to shoulder = splenic rupture, AAA (L1–2)**
   - Localized = lower acuity

5. **"On a 0–10 scale, how bad is it? Is it the worst belly pain you've ever had?"**
   - **7+ and acute = L2**
   - **"Worst ever" = L2**

6. **"Does anything make it better or worse? Walking? Lying down? Eating?"**
   - Worse with movement = peritoneal involvement (L2)
   - Improved by lying still = lower acuity
   - Worse with food = gastro-related (L3–4)

7. **"Are you feeling nauseous or vomiting?"**
   - Vomiting + severe pain = obstruction/acute condition (L2–3)
   - Mild nausea = lower acuity

8. **"Are you able to keep food/water down?"**
   - Unable to tolerate anything = dehydration risk (L2–3)
   - Can tolerate some = lower acuity

9. **"Any changes in bowel movements? Diarrhea, constipation, or blood in stool?"**
   - **Bloody diarrhea + fever = inflammatory/infectious colitis (L2–3)**
   - Constipation alone = lower acuity unless severe pain

10. **"Any vomiting blood or black/tarry stools?"**
    - **Hematemesis = GI bleed, L2**
    - **Melena = upper GI bleed, L2–3**

---

### Question Set 3.5: Infection/Fever Symptoms

**Ask if:** Fever, chills, or signs of infection present.

1. **"Have you had a fever? What was your temperature?"**
   - >38.3°C (101°F) = more concerning
   - Low-grade <38°C in well-appearing = lower acuity

2. **"Do you have chills?"**
   - **Chills = suggests bacteremia/serious infection, L2**

3. **"Do you have a rash? Can you describe it?"**
   - **Non-blanching petechial/purpuric rash = meningococcemia, L1–2**
   - Blanching rash = viral (lower acuity)
   - Localized rash = likely dermatitis (low acuity)

4. **"Where do you think you might have an infection? Any localized pain, redness, swelling, or discharge?"**
   - Localized infection sign = context for acuity

5. **"Are you feeling very weak, dizzy, or confused?"**
   - **Fever + weakness + confusion = sepsis, L1–2**

6. **"Have you had recent surgery, hospitalization, or medical procedures?"**
   - Recent invasive procedure + fever = infection risk (L2)

---

### Question Set 3.6: Trauma/Injury Symptoms

**Ask if:** Patient reports injury, fall, accident, or trauma.

1. **"Tell me about the injury. How did it happen?"**
   - High mechanism (high-speed MVC, fall >10 feet) = escalate acuity
   - Low mechanism (ground-level fall) = lower risk if stable

2. **"Were you unconscious at any point?"**
   - **Any LOC = L1–2**

3. **"Do you remember what happened?"**
   - Amnesia = head injury/TBI

4. **"Are you having any pain, swelling, or difficulty moving?"**
   - Severe pain = L2–3
   - Inability to move = L2

5. **"Any bleeding that won't stop?"**
   - Uncontrolled bleeding = L2
   - Controlled = lower acuity

6. **"Any numbness, tingling, or weakness in your arms or legs?"**
   - **New neurological symptoms = spinal injury concern, L2**

---

### Question Set 3.7: Psychiatric/Behavioral Symptoms

**Ask if:** Patient appears distressed, agitated, or behavior is concerning.

1. **"How are you feeling emotionally right now?"**
   - Distressed = assess further
   - Calm = lower psychiatric acuity

2. **"Have you been feeling sad, anxious, or depressed?"**
   - Assess severity and duration

3. **"Are you having thoughts of harming yourself or others?"**
   - **Yes to self-harm = L1–2**
   - **Yes to harm others = L1–2**
   - Assess for plan and access to means

4. **"Have you used any drugs or alcohol today?"**
   - Recent use = context for altered mental status (L2)
   - Overdose concern = L1–2

---

## Phase 4: Severity Assessment

**Goal:** Quantify how bad the symptoms are and validate perceived severity.

### Question Set 4.1: Pain Severity & Functional Impact

1. **"On a 0–10 pain scale, where 0 is no pain and 10 is the worst pain you've ever felt, where are you right now?"**
   - 0–3 = mild (L4–5)
   - 4–6 = moderate (L3–4)
   - 7–10 = severe (L2–3, especially if acute onset or abnormal vitals)
   - **Note: Patients often inflate pain scores; correlate with vital signs and behavior**

2. **"Is this pain keeping you from doing your normal activities?"**
   - Unable to do anything = severe (L2–3)
   - Can do some things = moderate (L3)
   - Not affecting activities = mild (L4–5)

3. **"How are you coping with this? Are you able to sit still, or do you need to move around?"**
   - Writhing/severe distress = high acuity
   - Lying quietly = may be severe but stable
   - Walking around = lower acuity pain

---

### Question Set 4.2: Symptom Severity Descriptors

**Ask patient to describe symptom using own words; listen for red-flag language:**

1. **"How would you describe the severity? Is it mild, moderate, severe, or unbearable?"**

2. **"If you had to compare it to a past experience, what would you compare it to?"**
   - "Worse than when I broke my arm" = severe
   - "Like my usual [symptom]" = familiar, lower risk

3. **"Has anything ever felt like this before?"**
   - **No = new symptom, higher acuity risk**
   - **Yes = may be lower acuity if same pattern**

---

## Phase 5: Red Flag Screening

**Goal:** Systematically screen for high-risk features that should escalate acuity.

### Question Set 5.1: Universal Red Flags

**Ask all patients:**

1. **"Since this started, have you experienced any of the following?"** (Read list)
   - Difficulty breathing
   - Chest pain or pressure
   - Severe headache
   - Confusion or difficulty thinking clearly
   - Loss of consciousness
   - Severe dizziness or spinning
   - Weakness on one side of your body
   - Difficulty speaking
   - Vision changes
   - Severe bleeding that won't stop
   - Difficulty swallowing
   - Fever over 38.3°C (101°F)
   - Chills
   - Rash that doesn't blanch (doesn't turn white when pressed)
   - Suicidal thoughts

   **Any "yes" = escalate to at least L2 and investigate further**

2. **"Are you having any difficulty breathing right now?"**
   - Yes = assess severity

3. **"Do you feel safe right now? Anyone hurting you?"**
   - Safety assessment for abuse/violence

---

### Question Set 5.2: Symptom-Specific Red Flag Screening

**For CHEST PAIN:**
- "Do you have a history of heart disease or heart attacks?"
- "Are you currently experiencing sweating, nausea, or shortness of breath along with the chest pain?"
- **Any yes = ACS protocol, L2**

**For HEADACHE:**
- "Is this the worst headache of your life?"
- "Do you have neck stiffness?"
- "Do you have a fever or rash?"
- **Any yes = consider meningitis/SAH, L1–2**

**For ABDOMINAL PAIN:**
- "Are you vomiting?"
- "Do you have a fever?"
- "Does the pain radiate to your back or shoulder?"
- **Any yes = L2**

**For FEVER:**
- "Are you having any confusion?"
- "Do you have a rash?"
- "Are you having severe chills?"
- **Any yes = L2**

**For WEAKNESS/FOCAL NEURO:**
- "Did this come on suddenly?"
- "Can you smile normally? Raise both arms equally?"
- "Can you speak normally?"
- **Any "no" or asymmetry = stroke protocol, L1–2**

**FOR ALTERED MENTAL STATUS:**
- "Are they their normal self?"
- "Is this acute change from baseline?"
- **Yes = L2, medical workup needed**

---

## Phase 6: Vital Sign Context

**Goal:** Integrate vital signs with symptom presentation.

### Question Set 6.1: Recent Changes & Symptoms

**After obtaining vital signs, ask:**

1. **"Have you noticed any recent changes in your heart rate? Do you feel like your heart is racing or beating irregularly?"**
   - Tachycardia + palpitations = arrhythmia (L2)

2. **"Do you feel lightheaded or dizzy when standing?"**
   - Orthostatic symptoms with vital changes = dehydration/hypovolemia (L2–3)

3. **"Are you having any swelling in your legs or hands?"**
   - May indicate cardiac/renal dysfunction (L2–3)

4. **"Have you had any recent weight changes or fluid gains/losses?"**
   - Context for dehydration/overload

5. **"Are your hands or feet cold or pale?"**
   - Poor perfusion signs = shock (L1–2)

6. **"Are you having night sweats or fever at home?"**
   - Suggests infection/systemic illness

---

## Phase 7: Risk Factors & Medical History

**Goal:** Contextualize symptom severity based on pre-existing conditions.

### Question Set 7.1: Chronic Conditions

1. **"Do you have any medical conditions we should know about? Such as:"**
   - Heart disease or arrhythmias
   - High blood pressure
   - Diabetes
   - Kidney disease
   - Lung disease (asthma, COPD)
   - Stroke or brain disease
   - Cancer or recent cancer treatment
   - Bleeding disorders or on blood thinners
   - Immune problems (HIV, transplant)
   - Recent surgery or hospitalization

   **Each comorbidity escalates concern; escalate acuity accordingly**

2. **"Are you on any medications? Can you tell me what they are?"**
   - **Beta-blockers, anticoagulants, immunosuppressants = escalate concern**
   - Medications may mask vital signs or increase risk

3. **"Do you have any drug allergies or medication allergies?"**
   - Needed for treatment decisions

4. **"Have you had any recent surgeries, procedures, or hospital stays?"**
   - Post-procedure complications (L2)
   - Recent chemotherapy = infection risk (L2)

---

### Question Set 7.2: Risk Factors for Specific Conditions

**For ACS risk:**
- "Do you smoke?"
- "Do you have high cholesterol or diabetes?"
- "Is there a family history of heart disease?"
- "Have you had a heart attack before?"
- **Multiple risk factors = escalate even with atypical presentation, esp. in women/elderly**

**For Stroke risk:**
- "Do you have high blood pressure?"
- "Do you have atrial fibrillation?"
- "Have you had a stroke before?"
- **Risk factors + any neurological symptom = stroke protocol, L2**

**For Sepsis risk:**
- "Do you have cancer or weakened immune system?"
- "Are you on chemotherapy or steroids?"
- "Have you recently been hospitalized or had surgery?"
- **Risk factors + fever = L2 sepsis protocol**

---

### Question Set 7.3: Medication & Substance Use

1. **"What medications are you currently taking?"**
   - **Anticoagulants (warfarin, DOACs) = bleeding risk, escalate concern**
   - **Beta-blockers = may mask tachycardia in shock**
   - **Immunosuppressants = infection risk**

2. **"When was the last dose of each medication?"**
   - Missed doses relevant for some conditions

3. **"Do you use alcohol? How much?"**
   - **Heavy use = withdrawal risk, malnutrition, liver disease (L2 if acute symptoms)**

4. **"Do you use any recreational drugs? When was the last use?"**
   - **Recent use = intoxication, overdose risk (L1–2 if acute)**
   - Cocaine use = ACS risk (L2)

---

## Phase 8: Elderly-Specific Questions

**Goal:** Account for atypical presentations in older adults (>65 years).

### Question Set 8.1: Geriatric-Specific Screening

1. **"Compared to your baseline, have you noticed any changes in your thinking, memory, or alertness?"**
   - **Acute confusion = delirium, may indicate serious illness (L2)**

2. **"Have you had any falls recently?"**
   - **Recent fall + altered mental status = head injury/intracranial bleed (L2)**

3. **"Have you been eating and drinking normally?"**
   - **Anorexia, decreased intake = may indicate serious illness (sepsis, MI, stroke)**

4. **"Have there been any changes in your ability to do daily activities?"**
   - Acute functional decline = serious illness

5. **"Do you live alone? Is someone helping you with your care?"**
   - Social support context for disposition/follow-up

---

## Phase 9: Women-Specific Questions

**Goal:** Screen for sex-specific presentations (especially ACS, pregnancy complications).

### Question Set 9.1: Women-Specific Screening

1. **"When was your last menstrual period?"**
   - Assess pregnancy status (relevant for abdominal pain, medication, imaging)

2. **"Could you be pregnant?"**
   - **Positive = escalate abdominal pain, dyspnea, chest pain (ectopic risk, PE, peripartum cardiomyopathy)**

3. **"Are you currently breastfeeding?"**
   - Context for medication safety

4. **"For chest discomfort: are you having any nausea, indigestion, or unusual fatigue?"**
   - **Women with ACS more likely to have atypical presentation (nausea, fatigue, SOB) without chest pain**

5. **"For chest discomfort: are you having pain between the shoulder blades or in your back?"**
   - **Women often report back/shoulder pain rather than chest pain in ACS**

---

## Phase 10: Pediatric-Specific Questions (if child patient)

**Goal:** Assess pediatric presentations (parents/caregivers provide history).

### Question Set 10.1: Pediatric Assessment

1. **"How is [child's name] acting compared to normal? Are they their usual self?"**
   - **Lethargy, decreased responsiveness = altered mental status, L2**
   - **Playful, interactive = reassuring**

2. **"When did the symptoms start?"**
   - Infants/neonates: **any fever = L2**
   - Older children: timeline of symptom onset

3. **"Have they had any fevers? What was the highest temperature?"**
   - **Fever <3 months = L2**
   - Fever + rash = meningococcemia risk

4. **"Are they eating/drinking normally or refusing intake?"**
   - Poor intake = dehydration (L2–3)

5. **"Have they had any falls or head injuries?"**
   - **Any LOC or behavioural change = head injury, L2**

6. **"Are they having any difficulty breathing?"**
   - Children can deteriorate rapidly

7. **"Do they look pale, blotchy, or are their extremities cold?"**
   - Poor perfusion = shock (L1–2)

8. **"For pain: is the child crying inconsolably, or are they their usual self?"**
   - Severity assessment

## Summary: Triage Flowchart by Questioning Response

**Use this decision tree based on answers:**

```
START: "What brings you in?"

├─ IMMEDIATELY LIFE-THREATENING signs?
│  └─ Unconscious, gasping, pulseless, severe bleeding, severe respiratory distress
│     └─ → L1 (RESUSCITATION) — Call Code, get help

├─ High-risk acute presentation?
│  ├─ Chest pain + cardiac features (diaphoresis, nausea, dyspnea, radiation)?
│  │  └─ → L2 (ACS protocol)
│  ├─ Sudden focal neurological deficit (weakness, speech, vision)?
│  │  └─ → L2 (Stroke protocol)
│  ├─ Severe headache + neck stiffness + fever?
│  │  └─ → L2 (Meningitis protocol)
│  ├─ Fever + petechial rash?
│  │  └─ → L1 (Meningococcemia — immediate antibiotics)
│  ├─ Acute altered mental status?
│  │  └─ → L2 (Workup for sepsis, stroke, metabolic, intoxication)
│  ├─ Sudden severe abdominal pain + radiating to back + hypotension?
│  │  └─ → L1–2 (AAA protocol)
│  └─ Suicidal/homicidal ideation with plan?
│     └─ → L2 (Safety)

├─ Potentially serious, needs assessment?
│  ├─ Moderate to severe pain (7+/10) with stable vitals?
│  │  └─ → L2–3
│  ├─ Fever + localised infection signs?
│  │  └─ → L2–3
│  ├─ New onset headache (severe, no red flags)?
│  │  └─ → L2–3
│  ├─ Moderate dyspnoea with SpO2 >90%?
│  │  └─ → L2–3
│  └─ High-mechanism trauma with stable vitals?
│     └─ → L2–3

├─ Needs evaluation, relatively stable?
│  ├─ Chronic condition with mild exacerbation?
│  │  └─ → L3–4
│  ├─ Mild to moderate pain (4–6/10) with normal vitals?
│  │  └─ → L3–4
│  ├─ Low-grade fever, well-appearing?
│  │  └─ → L4–5
│  └─ Isolated minor complaint (minor wound, sore throat, mild cough)?
│     └─ → L4–5

└─ Routine/non-urgent?
   ├─ Prescription refill?
   │  └─ → L5
   └─ Minor complaint, good function?
      └─ → L5
```

## Clinical Pearls for Triage Assessment

1. **Listen actively.** The patient's own words often reveal severity better than structured questions. Note distress in their voice, word choice, and emotional state.

2. **"Worst ever" is a red flag.** When patients say "worst pain/headache/symptom of my life," escalate to at least L2.

3. **Sudden onset = assume high acuity.** Ask clarifying questions, but sudden symptom onset should trigger higher acuity until proven otherwise.

4. **Normal vitals don't rule out serious illness.** Early sepsis, ACS, and stroke can present with normal vital signs. Rely on symptom assessment.

5. **Always ask about chest pain, headache, and difficulty breathing.** These are high-prevalence, high-acuity presentations that are easy to miss if not asked directly.

6. **Pay attention to elderly & women.** Both groups are at higher risk for atypical presentations and undertriage.

7. **Acute confusion is a medical emergency.** Always assume delirium from medical cause (sepsis, stroke, metabolic) until proven otherwise; psychiatric causes come later.

8. **Symptom clusters matter.** A patient with weakness + headache has worse outcomes than either symptom alone.

9. **Document exact wording when possible.** "Worst headache of my life," "sudden onset," "can't catch my breath" — these specific phrases guide acuity assignment.

10. **When in doubt, escalate.** It is safer to initially triage high and de-escalate later than to miss a serious condition.

## Example Triage Assessment Scripts

### Example 1: Chest Pain with Cardiac Features

**Q1:** "What brings you in today?"
**A:** "I have chest pain."

**Q2:** "When did it start?"
**A:** "About 30 minutes ago. Came on suddenly while I was sitting."

**Q3:** "Can you describe the pain? Sharp, dull, pressure, heaviness?"
**A:** "It's like pressure in my chest."

**Q4:** "Where is the pain? Does it radiate anywhere?"
**A:** "It's in the center of my chest and goes down my left arm."

**Q5:** "Are you having any sweating, nausea, or shortness of breath?"
**A:** "Yes, I'm sweating and feel nauseous."

**Q6:** "Do you have a history of heart problems or heart attacks?"
**A:** "No, but my father had a heart attack at 50."

**→ TRIAGE: L2 (ACS protocol) — stat ECG, cardiac monitoring, IV access**

---

### Example 2: Fever + Altered Mental Status in Elderly

**Q1:** "What brings you in?"
**A:** "My daughter called because I wasn't making sense this morning."

**Q2:** "When did you notice the confusion?"
**A:** "She says since this morning; I woke up confused."

**Q3:** "Have you had a fever or felt sick?"
**A:** "I don't know; I don't remember feeling sick."

**Q4:** "Have you had any falls or head injuries recently?"
**A:** "Not that I remember."

**Q5:** "Compared to baseline, are you more confused than usual?"
**A:** "Yes, daughter says I'm not making sense."

**→ TRIAGE: L2 (Sepsis/delirium protocol) — vital signs, labs, blood cultures, urinalysis**

### Example 3: Sudden Severe Headache

**Q1:** "What brings you in?"
**A:** "Worst headache of my life."

**Q4:** "Do you have a fever or rash?"

**→ TRIAGE: L1–2 (SAH/meningitis protocol) — stat CT head, neurology consult, prepare for lumbar puncture**

### Example 5: Low-Acuity Chronic Complaint

**Q1:** "What brings you in?"
**A:** "My back pain is acting up again."

**Q2:** "How long have you had back pain?"
**A:** "Years; I usually get flare-ups."

**Q3:** "Is this like your usual back pain?"
**A:** "Yes, pretty much the same."

**Q4:** "On a 0–10 scale, how bad is it?"
**A:** "About 5 or 6; it's annoying but not terrible."

**Q5:** "Any weakness, numbness, or difficulty walking?"
**A:** "No, I can walk fine."

**Q6:** "Any fever, bowel/bladder changes, or other symptoms?"
**A:** "No, just the back pain like always."

**Q7:** "What usually helps?"
**A:** "Heat and Ibuprofen."

**→ TRIAGE: L4 (chronic stable complaint) — pain management, PT referral**
